About

Signal Shrine is an interactive installation that reimagines oracle traditions through modern technology. Visitors approach a shrine of CRT televisions and draw physical NFC cards — each card triggers unique video effects, audio, and visual responses, creating a moment of digital divination.

The piece sits at the intersection of ancient ritual and contemporary technology, asking what it means to seek guidance in an age of screens and signals.

Concept

I've been thinking a lot about technology as ritual — how our interactions with devices have taken on qualities once reserved for sacred objects. We consult our phones like oracles, scrolling for signs, seeking answers in the feed.

Signal Shrine makes this implicit ritual explicit. The CRT televisions evoke both nostalgia and a kind of altar-like presence. The NFC cards are physical talismans — you hold them, choose them, offer them to the shrine. The video feedback and glitches become a form of digital prophecy.

It's playful but also sincere. There's something genuinely meaningful about creating space for wonder and mystery within technology.

Technical

The installation uses Arduino with RFID readers to detect which NFC card is presented. Each card has a unique ID that triggers specific responses in Max/MSP — video effects, feedback loops, sound design. The output displays on CRT televisions, whose inherent visual qualities (scan lines, warmth, slight blur) add to the ritual atmosphere.

  • Arduino with RFID/NFC readers
  • Custom NFC oracle cards
  • Max/MSP/Jitter for video processing
  • Real-time video effects and feedback
  • CRT televisions as display
  • Generative sound design

Process

This project brought together several threads in my practice — physical computing, video processing, and an ongoing interest in digital mysticism. The technical challenge was getting reliable communication between the Arduino RFID readers and Max/MSP, then designing video effects that felt both responsive and mysterious.

The CRTs were sourced from thrift stores and online listings — each one has its own character and quirks, which became part of the piece's personality.
